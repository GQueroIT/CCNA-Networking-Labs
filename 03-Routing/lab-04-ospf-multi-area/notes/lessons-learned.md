# Lessons Learned

This lab helped me understand how OSPF multi-area design begins to feel more like a real routed environment instead of a basic single-area setup. I saw how the network becomes more layered once multiple routers, user VLANs, and different OSPF areas are introduced into the same topology. That made me think more carefully about where routes were being learned, how traffic was flowing across the topology, and which router was acting as the border between areas.

One of the biggest things I reinforced in this lab was the importance of Area 0. I learned that OSPF multi-area design depends on Area 0 acting as the backbone, and the other areas must connect back to it properly. In this topology, that made router placement and network statements much more important than they were in earlier routing labs. I also got more comfortable identifying the Area Border Router and understanding why that device plays such an important role in exchanging routing information between areas.

This lab also helped me continue building confidence with point-to-point addressing using /30 networks. Those small subnets made the router-to-router links organized and efficient, and they also made it easier to verify whether each connection was configured correctly. I am starting to recognize that when the addressing plan is clean, troubleshooting becomes much easier because each interface has a very specific role in the topology.

Another important takeaway for me was seeing how router-on-a-stick can still be integrated into a larger routed design. Instead of treating VLAN routing and OSPF as completely separate ideas, this lab showed me how both can work together in the same environment. The VLAN traffic still needed local gateway configuration through subinterfaces, but OSPF was what allowed the rest of the routed network to learn how to reach those user subnets.

I also reinforced the habit of verifying configuration instead of assuming it is correct just because the commands were entered. Commands such as `show ip ospf neighbor`, `show ip protocols`, `show ip interface brief`, and `show vlan brief` made it easier to confirm whether the network was actually working the way I intended. This lab reminded me that successful networking is not just about entering commands. It is about being able to prove that adjacencies formed, routes were learned, trunks were carrying the correct VLANs, and interfaces were in the expected state.

This lab helped me move beyond simple routing practice and into a more structured enterprise-style design mindset. It showed me how switching, VLANs, trunking, router-on-a-stick, and OSPF can all connect together into one topology. More than anything, it pushed me to think more carefully about topology design, route advertisement, and verification, which is exactly the kind of progression I want as I continue building more advanced labs.
